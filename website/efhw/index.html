01 / Fundamentals

EFHW Fundamentals & Impedance Characteristics

The EFHW (End-Fed Half-Wave) antenna is essentially a half-wave dipole fed at one end. Compared to center-fed dipoles, its core advantages are simple deployment — only one support point needed, feed point near the radio, and harmonic-based multi-band coverage.

// Impedance Characteristics

  • ▸The end of a half-wave antenna presents extremely high impedance, typically 2000-3000 Ω (far above 50 Ω)
  • ▸Requires an impedance transformer: 49:1 (7:1 turns ratio, e.g. 3:21) or 64:1 (8:1 turns ratio)
  • ▸Actual impedance is affected by deployment height, environmental dielectric constant, ground conductivity, wire diameter, and other factors
  • ▸Current distribution: maximum at the center of the half-wave wire, zero at both ends — identical to dipole physics
  • ▸Feed point is a high-voltage, low-current node — 100W @ 2500Ω = 500V RMS / 700V peak

Key Physics Fact

The EFHW and dipole radiation patterns are nearly identical — both share the same current distribution. The EFHW is not a "magic antenna"; the only difference is the feed method and the resulting impedance matching challenge.

02 / Transformer Design +3:21

49:1 vs 64:1 Transformer Design & Selection

Antenna Actual Impedance ÷ 50 = Ideal Transformation Ratio.Actual impedance is ~2000-3000 Ω, and 49:1 provides a universal compromise. But identical turns ratios do not guarantee identical performance — PA3HHO's measurements reveal a critical, long-overlooked variable: more primary turns dramatically improve low-band efficiency.

PA3HHO Key Finding (2025)

The 3:21 winding is 5× more efficient on low bands than 2:14. The mechanism: more turns → higher inductive reactance → lower magnetizing current → dramatically lower core loss. On 80m, 3:21 loss is only 0.3dB (8%), while 2:14 reaches 2.3dB (41%). The trade-off is tighter winding space, which requires stacked cores to accommodate the extra turns.

Ferrite Core Selection

Research by RF.Guru and F5NPV reveals: no single core can maintain high efficiency across the entire 80m-10m range.

RF.Guru Warning

Type 43 efficiency on 10m drops as low as 55%; Type 52 on 80m reaches only ~50%. The so-called "wideband 80-10m EFHW transformer" is more marketing promise than reality.

PA3HHO Recommendations Practical Guide

  • ▸80/40m dedicated: 2×FT240-43, 3:21 winding (49:1), only 0.3dB (8%) loss
  • ▸20/15/10m dedicated: 2×FT240-52, 3:21 winding, optimal high-band efficiency
  • ▸Band-specific > all-band coverage — the most important design philosophy shift in this supplement
  • ▸2×43 3:21 handles 400W PEP SSB; 3×52 (N4LQ design) reaches 1kW PEP

03 / Winding Methods

Winding Methods & W8JI Measurement Corrections

Traditional Twisted-Pair

  • First 2 turns of primary & secondary twisted together
  • Remaining turns coupled via core flux
  • Has crossover winding

Fuchs Split-Winding

  • Secondary wound alone on core
  • Primary wound alone between secondary
  • Needs 0.05λ counterpoise

Simple Parallel Winding (Recommended)

  • Primary & secondary wound in parallel
  • W8JI measured: better on HF
  • Distributed capacitance adds CM impedance

05 / Counterpoise

Counterpoise — Balance Ground

Common Misconception (PA9X)

"A half-wave EFHW doesn't need a counterpoise" — WRONG. No dedicated counterpoise → coax shield → radio chassis → power supply → every appliance ground → entire house radiates RF.

Length Formula

L = 0.05 × λmax

40m→2m | 80m→4m | 160m→8m. Non-resonant length, sufficient to provide an RF return path without becoming a significant radiator.

AI6XG Minimalist Solution NEW

6ft RG316 coax = 0.05λ (40m). The coax segment between the transformer and CMC itself acts as the counterpoise — no extra wire needed.

Core Trade-off

  • • 2643251002 is more efficient, but ID is only 16.75mm, making 3:21 winding nearly impossible
  • • FT240-43 has ample space for 3:21; stacking 2 cores brings efficiency (~92%) nearly equal

09 / Stacked Measurements NEW

PA3HHO Stacked Core Measurement Comparison

PA3HHO (Pleun) conducted systematic measurements of four transformer configurations. This is the community's most comprehensive EFHW transformer efficiency comparison dataset.

Finding 1: Stacking Reduces Loss

2 cores: loss cut in half. 1×43→2×43, 80m from 2.3dB→0.8dB. Doubled cross-section → halved flux density → exponentially reduced loss.

Finding 2: 3:21 Efficiency Leap

Finding 3: Type 52 Triple Stack

Only 1% loss on 80m. But elevated VSWR and narrow bandwidth. N4LQ design reaches 1kW PEP.

Finding 4: Band-Specific Philosophy

43 for 80/40m, 52 for 20/15/10m. Band-specific optimization > all-band coverage.

10 / Measurement Myth NEW

Back-to-Back Measurement Myth — RF.Guru Critique

RF.Guru (ON6URE) published an article in November 2025 that fundamentally exposed the flaws of "back-to-back" testing.

Popular Method (WRONG)

[Radio 50Ω] → [Transformer A] → [Transformer B] → [Dummy Load 50Ω] "100W in, 94W out = 3W loss per transformer" "0.13dB ≈ nearly lossless!" ← This is a placebo

Why It's Wrong

Back-to-Back Ideal Conditions

  • • Perfect 50Ω at both ends
  • • Symmetric structure cancels CM
  • • Purely resistive, zero reactance

EFHW Real-World Conditions

  • • Load 2-5kΩ + reactance
  • • Reactive current circulates as heat
  • • SWR 3:1-5:1
  • • Coax shield = third conductor

"Back-to-back testing only proves the transformer survives in easy-mode. 500W without smoke ≠ 500W efficient radiation."

— ON6URE, RF.Guru (2025-11)

Correct Evaluation Method

  • • Use 2.4kΩ complex-impedance load (not purely resistive)
  • • RF current probe to measure coax shield common-mode current
  • • Temperature rise test at actual power + duty cycle

11 / VNA Methodology NEW

Correct VNA Measurement Method — AI6XG 3-Step Separation

AI6XG (Dan) proposed a rigorous methodology to decompose transformer loss into three independent components. This is key to distinguishing "bad transformer" from "antenna mismatch".

// AI6XG 3-Step Separation Method

Step 1: Mismatch Loss MM

MM = -10·log(1 - |S₁₁|²)

Reflection loss from impedance mismatch. Energy is reflected back to the transmitter, not turned into heat.

Step 2: Load Loss LL

LL = |S₂₁|² / (1 - |S₁₁|²)

Loss in the load resistor itself. Must first measure load complex impedance via Series-Through!

Step 3: Transformer Loss TL

TL = S₂₁ - MM - LL

The real transformer loss. This reflects the actual performance of the core, winding, and turns ratio.

Common Mistake

Assuming load is an ideal resistor → efficiency calculated >100% on high bands (absurd). Without measuring actual load complex impedance, MM and LL cannot be correctly separated, making transformer loss calculation meaningless.

AI6XG Key Findings

  • • Primary capacitance does not affect transformer efficiency — only affects mismatch loss MM
  • • Larger core = lower transformer loss. FT240 > FT140 > FT50
  • • NanoVNA-F + OSLIT calibration + fixture = repeatable amateur measurements

13 / Dual-Mode Matching NEW

A+C Dual-Mode Matching System — A New Direction for Low + High Bands

A traditional single 49:1 ferrite transformer fails at both 80m (insufficient inductance) and 10m (capacitive-dominant, efficiency collapses to 55%). The A+C approach decomposes the problem: ferrite wideband transformer for low bands, powdered-iron resonant coupler for high bands, each playing to its strengths.

15 / Radiation Patterns

Radiation Patterns & NEC Modeling

N6CC conducted in-depth NEC modeling analysis. The EFHW is the "last resort" for 80/60/40/30m regional coverage.

  • • 80m sloper 45°: 4 nulls in DX elevation, -25dB rear, -9dB sides
  • • Higher harmonics: deeper nulls, more lobes. 50ft: 8dB front null. 20ft: 10dB front null
  • • Inverted L: ground/sky wave interference → hot-spot dead zones → self-induced QSB
  • • NVIS at 6m height: 80/40m omnidirectional ±1dB, usable for 300km regional

"High gain, wide bandwidth, small size — pick two. The EFHW in practice delivers none of the three."

— N6CC (Tim)

17 / System Architecture NEW

EFHW Fuchs ATU V3.0 — Premium Architecture Board

The premium board splits the system into two real chains: the RF/DC physical path and the measurement/control loop. The ATR1000 sits explicitly behind the radio and before the Bias-T, at the shack-side reference plane; the remote ATU is only an actuator, and tuning quality is observed indirectly through the near-end SWR meter.

Topology accuracy

The ATR1000 is drawn as a near-end coupler on the RF main path — behind the radio, before the Bias-T — and is no longer mistakenly placed as a side sensor on the outdoor ATU.

Clear reference plane

Every SWR optimization is based on the radio-side reference plane. The remote ATU's effect reflects back through the long coax to this plane, where MRRC reads it.

Auditable control path

The RF physical path and the WebSocket control path are drawn as separate layers, exposing every latency / failure point: meter read, MRRC, WiFi, ESP32, servo.

No safety overclaim

The diagram deliberately keeps the "not directly detectable" boundary: capacitor arcing, tank heating, real servo position, current and water ingress cannot be substituted by ATR1000 sensing.
